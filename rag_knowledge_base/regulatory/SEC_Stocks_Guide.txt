=== STOCKS — FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS ===

WHAT ARE STOCKS?
Stocks are a type of security that gives stockholders a share of ownership in a company. Also called "equities."

Investors buy stocks for:
- Capital appreciation (when a stock rises in price)
- Dividend payments (when the company distributes some of its earnings)
- Ability to vote shares and influence the company

Companies issue stock to raise money for:
- Paying off debt
- Launching new products
- Expanding into new markets or regions
- Enlarging facilities or building new ones

TYPES OF STOCKS:

Common stock: Entitles owners to vote at shareholder meetings and receive dividends.
Preferred stock: Usually no voting rights but receives dividend payments before common stockholders; has priority over common stockholders if company goes bankrupt.

Additional categories:
- Growth stocks: Earnings growing faster than market average; rarely pay dividends; investors buy in hope of capital appreciation. (e.g., start-up tech companies)
- Income stocks: Pay dividends consistently; investors buy for income they generate. (e.g., established utility companies)
- Value stocks: Low price-to-earnings (PE) ratio; cheaper to buy than stocks with higher PE; may have fallen out of favor; investors hope the market has overreacted and price will rebound.
- Blue-chip stocks: Shares in large, well-known companies with solid history of growth; generally pay dividends.

By company size (market capitalization): Large-cap, mid-cap, small-cap, microcap, penny stocks.
- Penny stocks: Very lowest priced; little or no earnings; no dividends; highly speculative.

BENEFITS AND RISKS OF STOCKS:

Benefits:
- Greatest potential for growth (capital appreciation) over long haul
- Investors who stick with stocks over 15+ years generally have been rewarded with strong, positive returns
- Dividends provide income

Risks:
- Stock prices move down as well as up
- No guarantee company will grow and do well; can lose money invested
- If company goes bankrupt: common stockholders are LAST in line to receive proceeds (behind bondholders and preferred stockholders)
- Volatility: Large company stocks have lost money on average about one out of every three years
- Factors inside and outside the company can affect stock price

RISK MANAGEMENT:
- Offset risks by investing in a number of different stocks (diversification)
- Offset risks by investing in other assets that are not stocks (e.g., bonds)
- Long time horizon allows more ability to weather volatility
- Young investors saving for retirement may want more stocks than bonds; investors nearing retirement may want more bonds than stocks

STOCK CATEGORIES BY INVESTMENT STRATEGY:
- Direct stock plans (buy directly from company)
- Dividend reinvestment plans
- Discount or full-service broker
- Stock funds (mutual funds investing primarily in stocks)
